üèóÔ∏è Rebuilding the Nation

After the Civil War, leaders faced a huge task: restore the South, protect freedom, and rebuild the economy.

Constitutional Changes

  • 13th Amendment: ended slavery.
  • 14th Amendment: granted citizenship and equal protection.
  • 15th Amendment: protected voting rights for Black men.

Rebuilding Efforts

  • Freedmen‚Äôs Bureau helped with schools, jobs, and legal support.
  • New state governments reopened schools and repaired roads and railroads.
  • Families searched for relatives and built new communities.
  • Sharecropping offered land to farm, but high debt trapped many families.
  • Some laws limited freedom; later segregation reduced civil rights.

üè≠ Industrial Growth

New machines, power sources, and business ideas transformed production and travel.

Key Industries

  • Steel built bridges, railroads, and tall buildings.
  • Oil fueled machines, lamps, and later cars.
  • Electricity powered factories and cities.

Invention Spotlight

  • Telephone connected people over long distances.
  • Electric light extended working hours.
  • Typewriters and cash registers changed offices and shops.
  • Railroads moved goods faster and linked regions into a national market.
  • Corporations grew, and entrepreneurs built large businesses.

üèôÔ∏è Cities and Immigration

Millions of newcomers arrived, and cities expanded quickly to meet new demands.

  • People moved for jobs, land, and safety from conflict or poverty.
  • Many families lived in crowded tenements near factories.
  • Neighborhoods formed with shared languages, foods, and traditions.

Factory work created new opportunities, but also long hours and unsafe conditions.

  • Labor unions formed to negotiate wages, hours, and safety.
  • Strikes showed worker strength but could cause conflict.
  • Child labor and dangerous jobs led to calls for reform.
